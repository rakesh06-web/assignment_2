The table below outlines the key user interactions and the system's responses. This forms the foundation for assessing how well the site meets user expectations and supports core tasks.

The search capability utilizes Solr as its search engine to connect with Islandora for delivering fast and precise search outcomes. Users of the system can view documents directly through the browser or they can choose to download the files for offline use. Nielsen's heuristics: Guiding analysis of The Tower website (supporting the undergraduate teacher's lesson plan..)

Heuristic Issues
1. Visibility of System Status The database provided suitable results when users entered search terms which included “mental health,” “anxiety,” or “student well-being.” Users did not receive any visual notification during the search process that confirmed the system continued its operation. The search results displayed chronological organization although this sometimes failed to present the most suitable information. Users especially students who navigate under time pressure would benefit from search results sorted by relevance while also getting progress indicators such as “Loading search results".
2. Match Between System and Real World The presented results showed article thumbnails combined with titles and dates and the first sentence offerings which provided effective search support for students concerned about mental health subject matter. The interface design followed what users anticipated while enabling they could navigate the system in a natural way. The site contains terms such as "object" and "collection" yet these technical library or archival terminologies might confuse students who are not familiar with this specialized language. Everyday vocabulary choices instead of archival jargon such as “story” or “article” would help the tool reach more students.
3. User Control and Freedom The user is required to edit their search terms by hand or employ their browser's back function when no results match. This is basic but functional. The website does not provide search filter options to users who need to refine their queries nor does it show a recent search history for assistance in navigation. Users looking for information about mental health need easy pathways to recover from search mistakes to prevent aggravation.
4. Consistency and Standards Users can find the search bar consistently in the header section yet access to the archive navigation requires scrolling to the lower parts of the page. The platform lacks standard academic or news practices by using the beginning of each article as its summary while other platforms prefer segmenting content with keywords or summaries. Mentally unstable students may doubt their information source when image links within the website do not work properly which results in reduced credibility and confusion about content validity.
5. Error Prevention Users who enter “depression” incorrectly have no available assistance from the site because it does not provide either correction suggestions or appropriate alternatives. Users cannot perform search refinements through filters that include date selection or topic categories or article classification. Deeper topic exploration becomes difficult for students examining mental health because the platform does not provide enough assistance for sensitive research areas.
6. Recognition Rather Than Recall The chosen search phrase ("mental health") appears prominently displayed at the results page top after users make their search. The search term persists at the top of results for students to recall their original query. Students can easily switch between tasks due to the permanent navigation bar that appears at the top. The service doesn't offer visual topic categories or tags to assist students in identifying connected mental health subjects thus they must remember exact search terms.
7. Flexibility and Efficiency of Use The system doesn’t offer advanced search options or personalization features like saved searches or interest tags. A student researching mental health trends across time, for example, can’t filter by decade or issue. Flexibility features like these would improve the efficiency of research for educators and students working with thematic or long-term mental health projects.
8. Aesthetic and Minimalist Design Visitors experience cluttered results during search activities on this site because its layout presents a clean appearance. The homepage appears overcrowded because it contains an extensive number of visual elements and links. A topic focused on mental health requires an uncluttered design for research support because clarity and calmness aid the work.
9. Help users recognize, diagnose, and recover from errors When a search yields no results (e.g., searching for “suicidal thoughts”), the system doesn’t provide guidance. There are no suggested topics, alternative searches, or spelling corrections. For students or teachers exploring nuanced mental health terms, this can lead to dead ends and missed resources.
10. Help and documentation There is no visible help section or guide explaining how to use the archive or optimize search results. Teachers guiding students to explore mental health coverage in campus media would benefit from a simple “how to use the archive” tutorial or FAQ, especially since first-time users may be unfamiliar with archive systems.

Step 3: Summarize Part 1 with the top 2 strengths and top 2 weaknesses of the system

Top Strengths:

1. Responsive Search Functionality

The system provides immediate search results that pertain to "fast food" and "Catholic" every time a user types in these terms. The system presents results using article titles combined with thumbnail images and showing the first sentence of each article for quick user assessment of appropriate content. Users can quickly obtain search results thanks to a response system that functions well when working with extensive archives. Being an educator allows one to find needed materials without spending excessive time.

2. Organized Layout with Image Previews

Business researchers can easily understand search results because they display article titles along with publication dates and small pictures of each article. Users can quickly scan the content because of its well-structured layout to identify potential useful material. The top first sentence in previews helps users to find out if articles match their requirements swiftly. Undergraduate teachers benefit from this feature since it makes their search for original source material for educational purposes more efficient.

Top Weaknesses:

1. Lack of Advanced Search Features

A critical drawback of this website stems from its inadequate search functionality. The absence of search filters for date ranges and authors and topics in the site means searchers must manually examine numerous results to find specific archived content. Undergraduate teachers need to browse numerous results for "Catholic" symbolism articles from selected decades or authored by particular authors because the site lacks efficient search functions.

2. No Error Recovery or Suggestion Mechanisms

A search term with no results prompts users to experience an empty screen without any method for guidance. The system provides no assistance to users when they type less familiar search terms or spelling mistakes occur. The system creates frustration among users who lack experience with its interface because they lack the capability to improve their search or correct spelling errors.
